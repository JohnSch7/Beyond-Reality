Photogrammetry

Turning Real-Life Objects Into 3D Assets

Photogrammetry allows artists to reconstruct an objects shape, positioning, and materials through analyzing a set of photos taken at different angles. Photogrammetry takes the place of traditional hand modeling in 3D software. Mason Menzies, Photoscanning Manager at Poliigon relates photogrammetry to automated camera tracking. When trying to track the motion of a camera, software will often look for high detailed areas as reference for understanding the spatial positioning of a scene. Photogrammetry works very similarly. The process will attempt to track the same high detailed areas across a large dataset of images. This is how the shape and positioning of the object is reconstructed in a 3D space.
Although photogrammetry seems like a great tool for turning any real-life object into a 3D asset, there are some areas that fall short. For example, reflective and translucent materials are difficult to scan. The same can be said about objects that are smooth and pristine. The lack of details makes it hard for Photogrammetry to correctly process the object's positional data. Additionally, repeating patterns on an object can also confuse the processing. To give you an example, photogrammetry works better with something like a jagged rock with a bunch of detail, than a smooth white ceramic vase.
One of the reasons Call Of Duty: Modern Warfare II looks so realistic is because of how photogrammetry was used. In the real world, props were damaged to simulate wear and tear, then scanned into a digital workspace. This saved weeks of time and allowed for more precise details to be added. Instead of having multiple cameras taking pictures of a prop from different angles, the Arago rig utilizes one camera and automatically rotates the prop on a turntable.
