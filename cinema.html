The Future of Cinema

New Technologies in the World of Cinema

  • When you think of Unreal Engine you probably think of games. However, Unreal Engine is becoming increasingly used for virtual film production. In the video "The Future of Cinema" ICVFX Stage Operator and Epic Games employee Mike Sill gives an in depth look at how they pull off their virtual production. It all starts with a motion captured camera which is tied directly to Unreal Engine. This means it's possible to capture live footage with a digitally tracked environment as the background. The live recorded data gets sent to a physical control station as well where artists can do digital comp in real-time.

    One benefit to this approach of virtual film production is that the background and lighting will always be consistent. This means if an actor wore the wrong tie for instance, they could redo the scene months later with no other differences in the production. Additionally, there isn't a large film crew dragging their equipment to an inconvenient location while fighting against the sunlight.

  • The green screen has been used by filmmakers for decades to replace backgrounds and composite actors. Keying green screens isn't always as simple as a VFX artist may hope. For example, seams may stand out, and uneven lighting can cast different shades of green across the green screen. This then leads to an increase of manual labor. Fortunately, a new approach that utilizes LED panels could potentially be taking its place. What's being referred to as "the volume" is a wall of high resolution LED panels that wrap around a stage, with a matching physical set design, and 3D assets projected into the digital environment. The environment can also respond to camera movements, mimicking the real world.

    Charmaine Chan is credit as part of the "Brain Bar" Crew responsible for operating the volume on the set of The Mandalorian. A benefit to this workflow is that environments could be completely reworked within hours. Constant adjustments to 3D assets, animations, and color could be made in real-time. Another benefit is that post-production workers like Charmaine who would normally be editing in the corner of a room got the chance to interact with the gaffers, prop designers, set designers, and other crew members she wouldn't usually get to see.

  • This video by artist Lorenzo Drago is nothing short of visually stunning. It provides a look at what digital environments utilizing Unreal Engine 5 can look like. The train station is based on a real-life location in Toyama, Japan. It was built using 3D assets, but the scene can easily pass as real-life footage. Lorenzo makes use of Unreal Engine's global illumination system, Lumen. He also worked on all the modeling, texturing, lighting, and animation. The main goal of this project was to get as close to photorealism as possible. For the realistic camera movements, lorenzo used camera matching to get accurate proportions and reference.
  • Short film "Irradiation" written and directed by Sava Zivkovic is a 3D animated short made with tools including Unreal Engine, Blender, Zbrush, Substance Painter, and Adobe Suite. A motion capture facility was used for the animation data of the characters. This short features amazing storytelling, voice acting, and incredible virtual production.
